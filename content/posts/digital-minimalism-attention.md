In 1971, Herbert Simon — Nobel laureate, polymath, one of the founders of artificial intelligence — wrote something that has only grown more prescient with each passing year:

> "A wealth of information creates a poverty of attention."

Fifty-five years later, we are drowning in information and starving for attention. The average person checks their phone 150 times per day. Each check is a context switch. Each context switch carries a cognitive cost that compounds silently throughout the day.

We have traded depth for breadth, and we are intellectually poorer for it.

## The Attention Economy

Your attention is not just valuable to you — it's valuable to every company competing for it. The business model of the modern internet is, at its core, attention extraction. Social media feeds are not designed to inform or connect you. They are designed to maximize engagement, which is a polite way of saying they are designed to make it difficult.

This is not a conspiracy theory. It's a business model, documented extensively by the engineers who built these systems. Tristan Harris, formerly of Google, has been particularly eloquent on this point: these platforms exploit the same psychological vulnerabilities that slot machines do — variable reward schedules, social validation, and the fear of missing out.

## The Cost of Fragmentation

Cal Newport's research on deep work demonstrates that cognitively demanding tasks — the kind that produce real value — require sustained, uninterrupted focus. The threshold appears to be around 60-90 minutes of unbroken concentration before you reach the state of cognitive flow where your best work happens.

Consider what this means in practice. If you check your phone every ten minutes, you never reach flow state. Not once. You spend your entire day in cognitive shallows, skimming the surface of every task without ever diving deep.

The result is a peculiar modern exhaustion: the feeling of having been busy all day while accomplishing nothing of substance.

## A Framework for Reclamation

Digital minimalism isn't Luddism. It doesn't require abandoning technology. It requires *intentionality* — using technology as a tool rather than being used by it.

**Audit ruthlessly.** For every app on your phone, every notification enabled, every service you use, ask: does this directly support something I deeply value? If the answer is "sort of" or "sometimes," that's a no.

**Batch communication.** Email, messages, and social media do not require real-time engagement. Process them in defined windows — twice or three times a day — and protect the time between those windows ferociously.

**Protect your mornings.** The first hours of the day are when your cognitive resources are freshest. Spending them on email and social media is like using premium fuel to idle in a parking lot.

**Embrace boredom.** The compulsion to fill every idle moment with stimulation — checking your phone in line, scrolling during meals — erodes your capacity for sustained attention. Boredom is not the enemy; it's the environment in which your best ideas emerge.

## The Deeper Point

This isn't really about phones or apps. It's about sovereignty — the question of who controls your mind's allocation of its most precious resource.

Every moment of attention you give to something is a moment you *don't* give to something else. When you let algorithms decide where your attention goes, you're outsourcing life's most consequential decision.

The reclamation of attention is not a productivity hack. It's an assertion of autonomy. And in an age engineered to erode that autonomy, it's one of the most important choices you can make.
